What Are Corals?

Corals are ancient and unique animals. They are closely related to jelly fish and sea anemones.

A coral is a colonial organism, which means it comprises of many polyps.

A polyp is a small organism that has a mouth at one end that is surrounded by tentacles. As polyps grow and die, new ones grow on top of them creating common coral structures.

The tentacles are used for a variety of functions including capturing small creatures and defense. They feed using nematocysts, stinging cells, much like jellyfish have. It is a toxin that can be lethal and allows them to capture their food.

They have a very delicate symbiotic relationship with algae, known as zooxanthellae, that live on them. The algae live in the protection of the coral’s digestive cavity. They use coral waste to photosynthesize and provide the coral with carbohydrates which help them build their structure.

Types of Coral

The coral that forms reefs are known as stony coral; they are the ones with the calcium carbonite structures. The common shapes of coral are: massive, tabular, branching, encrusting, foliaceous, and columnar.

Where Coral is Found

Corals are found in very specific areas. Reef-growing species are found in shallow, warm water specifically around 70-85 degree waters between 60 and 90 feet deep. They can only live in salt water and tend not to survive near river openings or areas where freshwater runoff occurs. There are many other factors that create an optimal habitat for coral such as a rock ground from the to attach onto and the presence of prey.
Note: There are other types of coral that can live in deep waters with lower temperature and less light.

Coral Reefs are Worth Billions

Corals provide many important benefits to people from shoreline protection to medicines to biodiversity. From an economic perspective coral simply existing is worth billions of dollars.

Coral Reefs are a Classic Tourist Activity

Snorkeling and scuba diving of the coast is a classic tourist activity. Reefs provide tourists the opportunity to see hundreds of species of marine life all in one place. Estimates suggest tourists spend $11.5 billion globally on coral reef related tourism.

Coral Reefs Break Wave Energy

Coral reefs protect coastlines because they dissipate wave energy. They can absorb up to 97% of the wave energy. Estimates suggest this is worth $10.7 billion. This is measured by replacement costs (using a man-made structure instead of reefs for protection) or by damages avoided.

Coral Reefs Are Home to Commercially Important Fish

Fisheries benefit from reefs because they are the natural habitat of many commercial important species. Fisheries provide fish that are sold to be eaten as well ornamental aquarium fish. Coral reef fishing is worth $6.8 billion.

Climate Change leads to Bleaching

Corals can only survive in very narrow range of water temperature. The increase in temperature means the water is too hot for them to survive in.

The warmer temperature leads to coral bleaching. When the water is too warm the coral expels the algae they are hosting and causes them to turn white. The addition of CO2 in the air is absorbed by the ocean making it more acidic. This dissolves the calcium carbonate skeletons of corals.

Pollution leads to Algae Blooms

When it rains nutrients such as sugarcane runoff gets into the water and causes algae blooms. Too much algae in the water is harmful to coral because it causes algae blooms that smother the coral. It also blocks sunlight preventing the algae hosted by the coral from photosynthesizing.

Pollution can come from sunscreen on snorkelers and divers in the area. The increase in coral disease has been directly attributed to human activity.

Careless Destruction of Coral

Coral mining is when parts of coral are removed for making jewelry or to put in aquariums.

Irresponsible divers and snorkelers stand on the coral and destroy it.

Dredging for the purpose of building an oceanfront restaurant or to bring in cruise ships means destroying any coral in that area.

Grounding boats colliding with coral also leads to their destruction.

Overfishing and Other Destructive Fishing Practices Harm Coral

Overfishing is one problem that dirstrupts the balance of the ecosytem. Removing herbivorous fish from the ecosystem either directly or indirectly means the are no longer feeding on the algae. When the algae overgrows it suffocates the coral.

Destructive fishing practices also take a toll on coral.

Cyanide fishing is a practice where fishers use cyanide to stun or kill catch but it also kills coral.

Dynamite fishing is popular in Southeast Asia where it is intended to target the fish, but destroys large amounts of coral at the same time.

Dredging is another common fishing practice that destroys thousand year old reefs. A fishing dredge is dragged along the ocean floor and damages coral as it is dragged over it.

Restoration Efforts at UM

Coral gardening is the process of growing coral to put back into reefs to help restore reef ecosystems that are dying. The efforts began at the University of Miami in 2007 when the coral nursery was established. Since 2012 UM has planted approximately 600 meters of healthy coral back into the reef. The nursery has about 1,500 corals to be used for the purposes of restoration.
